Takakazu Ishii Sensei

Name
Takakazu Ishii
Born
January 1949, Tokyo; currently residing in Ibaraki Prefecture
Iaido
Kyoshi 8th Dan
Kendo
Kyoshi 7th Dan

His journey into Iaido began with his encounter with Shizufumi Ishido Sensei 65 years ago. He was a 12-year-old middle school student, and Ishido Sensei was about 16 years old, a high school student. They both entered a school affiliated with Nihon University, which allowed students to advance to university, and joined the same Kendo club.

Ishii Sensei was a beginner in Kendo, but Ishido Sensei was already a 3rd Dan and taught him many things. A year later, he was able to participate in matches and joined the team as a competitor when the dojo run by Ishido Sensei's father participated in a tournament in Kawasaki City.

When he first visited the Shinbukan Ishido Dojo, he became interested in the sword hanging on the wall. It was then that Sadataro Ishido Sensei, the father of Shizufumi Sensei, taught him about Iaido, a martial art using a sword, and he was taught Muso Shinden-ryu Shoden.

Until graduating from high school, he only occasionally practiced alone with a wooden sword to keep his Shoden skills in check; he focused solely on Kendo and did not seriously practice Iaido.

When he entered Nihon University, he learned about the Iaido Club and chose Iaido over Kendo. The Nihon University Iaido Club was based at the training hall of Tomoaki Danzaki Sensei, a 9th dan Iaido master, so he seriously began practicing Iaido at the age of 18 as a first-year university student.

Many distinguished masters from across the country, including Ishido Sadataro Sensei, came to the training hall for practice, and Ishii Sensei was fortunate to receive a great deal of instruction. At that time, there were only about five universities in the Tokyo area that were active in Iaido. Looking to the future, he and his colleagues united these five universities to develop student Iaido, and took the lead in establishing the Kanto Student Iaido Federation, laying the foundation for the current All Japan Student Iaido Federation.

After graduating from university and obtaining 5th Dan, his work became very busy. At the same time, he got married and became so preoccupied with daily life that he was away from Iaido practice for about 15 years.

After a 20-year hiatus, he returned to Kendo and achieved 7th Dan. This led to a request from a local Kendo practitioner for instruction in swordsmanship, prompting his return to Iaido. He aimed for 6th Dan, but the 15-year gap proved unforgiving. The Seitei Iaido he was learning was completely different from the early versions he had studied: the original only had seven forms, and he had no idea about the 8th, 9th, and 10th. Naturally, he failed the 6th Dan exam.

On the fourth attempt, he happened to meet Ishido Sensei, who was at the venue to support a student. Ishido Sensei said, "What era's Seitei Iaido are you using? Come to the dojo if you'd like." That was 23 years ago.

Ishii Sensei revered Ishido Sensei as his teacher, and every Tuesday he spent about three hours at the dojo, receiving thorough instruction from the very first Seitei Iaido form. He obtained 6th Dan in June 2000.

In July of the same year, he won the 6th Dan division at the Ibaraki Prefecture Kendo Federation Iaido Tournament, his first time participating, and represented Ibaraki Prefecture in the 6th Dan division at the 37th All Japan Iaido Tournament in Oita. The joy of participating as a representative of Kanagawa Prefecture from Ishido Dojo, alongside Morishima Sensei and other 5th, 6th, and 7th Dan practitioners, is a special memory.

After obtaining 7th Dan, he had the opportunity to teach Kendo and Iaido in Berlin, Germany. This led to an invitation to teach Kendo and Iaido in Szczecin, Poland, and later in the Republic of North Macedonia. He was asked to teach by the Kendo and Iaido Federation of the Republic of North Macedonia, and his visits ended when the European Kendo Championships were held there, as he felt that the foundation for Kendo and Iaido in the country had been established.

Most recently, he attended a Kendo and Iaido seminar in Ruse, Bulgaria, two years ago. Last October, he was invited to an Iaido seminar in Valencia, Spain, and with Ishido Sensei's formal permission, he visited as an instructor.

Inspired by his Iaido friends from university days being promoted to 8th Dan one after another, he decided to challenge for 8th Dan before turning 70. He asked Ishido Sensei for help and began rigorous training towards 8th Dan. At this point, he regretted having taken a long break from training.

Just as he had fulfilled the required years of training and was ready to challenge for 8th Dan, a scandal occurred in the Iaido world, and the examination was canceled. Perhaps because of this, his heightened motivation waned, and he suffered a myocardial infarction, requiring hospitalization and surgery. The following year, he made his first attempt at 8th Dan in Kyoto, passing the first examination but ultimately failing.

From the following year, the 8th Dan examination was held twice a year, in Kyoto and Tokyo. Although he attempted it each time, he only passed the first stage. He thought, "My ability is only enough to pass the first stage," so he decided to make the next attempt his last. That was the Kyoto examination last May.

Up until then, he had wanted to pass and be promoted, but perhaps because he had given up on that goal, when he went to Ishido Sensei for advice after passing the first stage, Ishido Sensei told him, "Go to the second stage with the same mindset." This made him feel much more at ease and allowed him to concentrate on his performance.

When the results were announced, from a distance, he could only see the numbers of four people. He resigned himself to the fact that he only had the ability to pass the first stage. But when he looked at the list, to his delight, his number was there.

The joy of passing the 8th Dan examination is exceptional, but Ishido Sensei told him to pursue his own Iaido as an 8th Dan. So, he still commutes to Kawasaki for rigorous training to become a true 8th Dan.
